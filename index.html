Typo—what?

We are all facing those tiny typographic challenges every day. Knowing how to master them, will not only make you a better human being and earn you the respect of the typo nerds.—No, it will also make your texts more legible, your slides more impressive and will help getting your messages across in a more beautiful way. The shortcuts work on Mac.

Fonts vs. Typefaces

A common misunderstanding is that fonts and typefaces are the same. Most people (incl. a lot of the designers) nowadays refer to typefaces as fonts. Here are two quotes that explain the difference in a very clear way:

“The physical embodiment of a collection of letters, numbers, symbols, etc. (whether it’s a case of metal pieces or a computer file) is a Font. When referring to the design of the collection (the way it looks) you call it a Typeface.”
(Marc Simmons, fontfeed.com)

“The way I relate the difference between typeface and font to my students is by comparing them to songs and MP3s, respectively (or songs and CDs, if you prefer a physical metaphor).”
(Nick Sherman, fontfeed.com)

(All sorts of) Dashes

Here is a really long Wikipedia article of how to use various dashes. There are more kinds of dashes than those mentioned here but these are the ones you’re most likely to use and confuse.

A Hyphen
You know how to make this. Used to break single words into parts (like when lines of type break within a word) or to join separate words into single words.
I love me some hand-lettering.

An en dash
Used in dates to replace “to” or “and”. It can also be used to illustrate the relationship between two different words. In German also used for a break of thought, with a space before and after the en dash.
I was in jail from 1976–1978.
I have a love–hate relationship with stretchy denim.

An em dash
For a break of thought similar to but stronger than a thought contained within parentheses. An em dash can also be used where a period (or full-stop) seems too strong but a comma seems too weak. At times it can have a similar vibe to a colon. The em dash is uncommon in the German language and most common in American type setting.
I once had to use the bus station bathroom—horrifying.
I tried a durian fruit once—tasted like hamburger pudding.
